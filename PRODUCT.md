# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Two equal audiences, one site:

**Hiring managers and recruiting leads** at product companies seeking a senior IC engineer or lead. They're skimming dozens of portfolios; they'll spend 45 seconds deciding whether to read further. They want signal: technical depth, taste, the ability to ship things that matter.

**Startup founders and operators** considering freelance or studio-rate collaboration. They're looking for someone who builds with care, understands product problems, and communicates clearly. They need to trust the work before they reach out.

Both audiences land at the same site. The portfolio must speak to both without being diluted for either.

## Product Purpose

Personal portfolio for Alex Waddell. The job is to start conversations: an email from a hiring manager, a DM from a founder, a reply from a collaborator. Not to close the deal — to earn the first message.

Success: visitors who care about craft, collaboration, and quality decide this is worth following up.

## Brand Personality

Curious, craft-obsessed, playful.

The playfulness is specific: it lives in the details (easter eggs, interactive experiments, glitch animations on name click) not in the layout. The layout can be precise and confident while the moments of delight are hidden and discovered. Linear meets someone who also builds a snake game behind a Konami code.

Tone of voice: direct, warm, no fluff. Shows rather than tells. The labs section is the strongest expression of curiosity — experiments that exist because he wanted to know if he could build them.

## Anti-references

- **Generic dev portfolio template**: big hero with avatar, cycling role text, "I'm a developer who loves..." intro paragraph, a skills grid of logo icons, identical project cards. The format is the problem, not just the styling.
- **AI-generated design output**: gradient text clips, glassmorphism cards, hero metric blocks, identical card grids, gradient-heavy CTAs. If the design could have been prompted out of Midjourney or v0 with a generic prompt, it's failed.
- **Over-animated portfolio showreel**: everything animated, particles, WebGL, style-over-substance. Impressive for 10 seconds, exhausting after that.
- **Dry digital CV**: just a resume with a theme applied. No personality, no evidence of what it's like to work with you.

## Design Principles

1. **Show, don't tell.** The lab experiments and case studies are the argument. Copy describes, they demonstrate.
2. **Earn every element.** Sparse like Linear: if something doesn't carry weight, remove it. No section exists to fill space.
3. **Personality lives in the details.** Precision at the layout level; delight encoded into interactions, easter eggs, micromoments. Not the reverse.
4. **Both audiences find their signal.** Depth for the hiring manager (systems thinking, technical work, scale). Taste for the client (visual quality, product sense, communication evidence).
5. **The bar is your own work.** You build interfaces for others that care about UX. The portfolio must clear the same bar — no excuses for sloppy type, broken rhythm, or generic components.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA. Keyboard navigation throughout, semantic HTML, visible focus rings, screen reader support on interactive elements. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` for all animations.
